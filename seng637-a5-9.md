# Assessment Using Reliability Growth Testing

### **Result of range analysis**

##### **1. Trend Analysis using the Laplace Factor**

The **Laplace Factor (u)** is a statistical test used to determine if a software system is showing reliability growth. It evaluates the distribution of failures over a testing period \[0, T].

- **Negative Score (u < 0):** Indicates **Reliability Growth** (the rate of failures is decreasing).

- **Positive Score (u > 0):** Indicates **Reliability Decay** (the rate of failures is increasing).

- **Score below -2:** Provides strong statistical evidence (at the 95% confidence level) that the system is significantly improving.

##### **2. Laplace Factor Calculations**

Based on the provided formula for failure count data, the Laplace factor is calculated as follows:

$$
u = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} (i-1) \cdot n(i) - \frac{k-1}{2} \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{k} n}{\sqrt{\frac{k^2-1}{12} \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{k} n}}
$$

Where:

- $u$ = Laplace factor
- $k$ = number of intervals
- $\sum_{i=1}^{k} n$ = total number of failures
- $n(i)$ = number of failures in interval $i$
- $\sum_{i=1}^{k} (i-1) \cdot n(i)$ = weighted sum of failures

For the time period \[0, T], divided into k units of equal length, and for n(i) be the number of failures observed during the time interval i.

### **A discussion on the advantages and disadvantages of reliability growth analysis**

Reliability growth analysis (RGA) provides a principled statistical framework for quantifying the evolution of system reliability during testing. By explicitly modelling the failure process over time, it enables both **inference on defect dynamics** and **prediction of future reliability states**, making it a central tool in software reliability engineering.

### **Advantages**

A primary strength of RGA lies in its ability to capture **temporal reliability evolution**. Unlike static metrics, failure intensity functions and mean value functions provide a continuous representation of how defect discovery and correction impact system behaviour. This enables a **mechanistic understanding of reliability improvement**, rather than a purely descriptive assessment.

RGA also offers **predictive capability through parametric modelling**. Established formulations (e.g., NHPP-based models, Goel–Okumoto family, covariate-augmented models) allow extrapolation beyond observed data, supporting forward-looking decisions such as release readiness, residual defect estimation, and testing horizon planning. The inclusion of covariates further enhances model expressiveness by linking reliability to operational factors (e.g., execution effort, debugging intensity), enabling **context-aware reliability assessment**.

Another advantage is the **statistical rigour underlying model selection and evaluation**. Criteria such as AIC, BIC, and likelihood-based measures provide objective mechanisms for selecting models that balance explanatory power and parsimony. This facilitates reproducible and defensible decision-making grounded in quantitative evidence.

Finally, RGA supports **data-driven optimization of testing strategies**. By revealing phases of rapid defect discovery versus diminishing returns, it enables more efficient allocation of testing resources and informs decisions on when additional testing yields marginal benefit.

### **Limitations**

Despite its analytical strengths, RGA is inherently **sensitive to data quality and segmentation**. Early-phase testing data often exhibit non-stationary or noisy behaviour, and inclusion of such data without proper validation (e.g., via Laplace trend analysis) can bias parameter estimation and degrade predictive accuracy.

RGA is also **model-dependent**, and its conclusions are contingent on the appropriateness of the selected reliability model. Different model structures can yield divergent estimates of failure intensity and residual defect counts, introducing epistemic uncertainty. This is particularly pronounced when the underlying failure process deviates from model assumptions (e.g., non-Poisson behaviour, abrupt changes in testing intensity).

According to the lecture slides, reliability models should only be used on data where the **overall reliability is increasing** as testing continues.

A further limitation is the implicit assumption of **homogeneity in the failure process**. In practice, software systems often undergo evolving operational profiles, varying test coverage, and heterogeneous defect classes, which can violate model assumptions and limit generalizability.

Additionally, RGA typically requires **sufficient failure data to achieve statistical stability**. In low-failure regimes or early testing stages, parameter estimates may be unstable, reducing confidence in both inference and prediction.

Finally, while RGA is effective for modelling test-phase behaviour, it does not inherently guarantee **operational reliability equivalence**, as real-world usage conditions may differ from controlled testing environments. Consequently, predictions must be interpreted within the context of the testing regime.

### **Synthesis**

Reliability growth analysis is most powerful when applied to **statistically validated, sufficiently rich datasets**, where it can yield both **robust inference and actionable predictions**. However, its effectiveness depends critically on **appropriate model selection, careful data segmentation, and disciplined interpretation**, particularly when extending conclusions beyond the observed testing domain.

# Assessment Using Reliability Demonstration Chart

## Overview

The Reliability Demonstration Chart (RDC) provides a graphical method to determine whether a system under test (SUT) meets its target Mean Time To Failure (MTTF) based on observed failure data. Unlike reliability growth testing, which focuses on modeling trends and predicting future behavior, RDC is a threshold-based decision tool that classifies system reliability into three regions: Accept, Continue Testing, or Reject.

## Data Preparation and Transformation

The original failure data was provided in grouped interval format (failures per time interval). To make this compatible with RDC analysis, which requires time-between-failure data, we transformed the dataset through the following process:

### Transformation Methodology

1. **Uniform Distribution Assumption**: For each interval with multiple failures, we assumed failures were uniformly distributed within that interval.

2. **Time-Between-Failures Calculation**: For interval *i* with *n* failures:
   - Time between failures (TBF) = 1 / *n*
   - Each failure in that interval is assigned TBF = 1 / *n*

3. **Cumulative Calculations**:

   - Cumulative failure count increments by 1 for each failure
   - Cumulative time increments by TBF for each failure

4. **Zero-Failure Intervals**: Intervals with 0 failures (intervals 10, 14, 25, 28) contribute to cumulative time but add no failure events.

**Example**: Interval 2 had 11 failures in 1 time unit

- TBF = 1/11 ≈ 0.091
- This creates 11 individual failure events, each with TBF = 0.091
- Total time contribution: $11 \times 0.091 = 1.0$

### Transformation Results

The transformation yielded:

- **92 individual failure events** (expanded from 31 intervals)
- **Total cumulative time**: 31.0 time units
- **Observed MTTF**: 31 / 92 = **0.337 intervals/failure**

### Limitations of Transformation

This transformation introduces several assumptions that should be considered when interpreting results:

- **Uniform distribution** of failures within intervals may not reflect actual failure clustering
- **Discrete approximation** of a continuous failure process
- **Information loss** from aggregated data

## Risk Profile Selection

For this analysis, we used **Risk Profile I** with the following parameters:

- **Discrimination Ratio (γ)**: 2.0
- **Developer's Risk (α)**: 0.10 (10% chance of rejecting an acceptable system)
- **User's Risk (β)**: 0.10 (10% chance of accepting an unacceptable system)

These parameters define the boundaries between Accept, Continue Testing, and Reject regions on the RDC chart. The discrimination ratio γ = 2.0 means the acceptable MTTF is twice the minimum acceptable MTTF.

**Methodological Note**: The RDC boundary formulas used by our analysis tool create distinct Accept and Reject boundaries (with a visible Continue Testing region between them). This differs from standard exponential RDC formulations where equal α and β values produce overlapping boundaries. The specific boundary calculations are tool-dependent.

## Determination of MTTFmin

### Methodological Limitation: Exploratory Framework

A fundamental limitation of our analysis is that we selected **MTTFmin = 0.337 intervals/failure** based on the **observed system performance** (31 intervals / 92 failures). This represents an **exploratory** rather than **confirmatory** approach:

**Circular Reasoning Challenge**:

- We are testing: "Does the system meet the threshold it already demonstrated?"
- By definition, the answer is predetermined
- This limits RDC's utility as a true **decision tool**

**Why This Approach Was Necessary**:

In the absence of:

- Predetermined reliability requirements
- Stakeholder-defined MTTF targets
- Industry-specific standards
- Operational performance specifications

We adopt an **exploratory sensitivity analysis** to understand:

1. How the system performs relative to its own baseline (0.337)
2. Whether reliability is robust or marginal when requirements vary
3. What safety margin exists above minimum demonstrated performance

### Interpretation Framework

For this analysis, the three MTTF scenarios should be interpreted as:

- **Half MTTFmin (0.169)**: "What if requirements were twice as stringent as observed performance?"
- **MTTFmin (0.337)**: "Does the system consistently meet its observed average performance?"
- **Double MTTFmin (0.674)**: "Does the system exceed its baseline by a comfortable margin?"

**Note for Practical Deployment**: A production RDC analysis should use requirement-driven MTTFmin values derived from:

- Failure cost analysis
- Service level agreements (SLAs)
- User impact assessments
- Regulatory or safety standards

## Advantages and Disadvantages of RDC

### Advantages

1. **Simplicity and Visual Clarity**
   - Graphical representation makes acceptance decisions intuitive
   - No complex statistical modeling required
   - Easy to communicate results to non-technical stakeholders
   - Visual trajectory provides narrative of testing progression

2. **Real-Time Decision Making**
   - Can be updated incrementally as new failure data arrives
   - Provides immediate feedback on testing progress
   - Supports dynamic test planning ("continue" vs "stop" decisions)
   - Enables adaptive test strategies based on current trajectory

3. **Risk Quantification**
   - Explicitly incorporates developer's risk (α) and user's risk (β)
   - Allows stakeholders to balance false acceptance vs false rejection
   - Transparent risk trade-offs through discrimination ratio (γ)
   - Confidence levels are built into boundary calculations

4. **Low Data Requirements**
   - Effective even with limited failure data (compared to parametric models)
   - Does not require complex parameter estimation
   - Suitable for early testing phases
   - Robust to small sample sizes

5. **Clear Go/No-Go Criteria**
   - Binary decision framework (Accept/Reject/Continue)
   - Removes ambiguity in release readiness assessment
   - Aligns testing activities with business objectives
   - Provides defensible decision trail

6. **Historical Validation**
   - Well-established method with decades of use (MIL-HDBK-781)
   - Validated across multiple domains (military, aerospace, software)
   - Standardized risk profiles available
   - Industry acceptance and familiarity

### Disadvantages

1. **Lack of Predictive Power**
   - Does not forecast future reliability or failure trends
   - Cannot estimate residual defect counts or predict when next failure will occur
   - Provides no insight into reliability growth trajectory or improvement rate
   - Limited forward-looking guidance for test planning beyond "continue/stop"
   - Cannot extrapolate to post-release operational reliability

2. **Sensitivity to MTTF Selection**
   - Acceptance decision heavily depends on chosen MTTF threshold
   - No inherent mechanism to determine "correct" or optimal MTTF
   - Different MTTF values can yield contradictory conclusions (as demonstrated in our three scenarios)
   - Requires external justification for MTTF target
   - Vulnerable to arbitrary or politically-motivated threshold setting

3. **Binary Decision Framework Limitations**
   - Oversimplifies complex reliability assessment into three regions
   - Does not capture nuanced reliability behavior or provide gradations of quality
   - Borderline cases may be ambiguous or misclassified
   - No quantitative measure of "how acceptable" the system is (only yes/no)
   - Loss of information compared to continuous reliability metrics

4. **Assumption of Constant Failure Rate**
   - RDC fundamentally assumes exponential distribution (constant hazard rate)
   - Real systems often exhibit time-dependent failure patterns (bathtub curve)
   - Ignores reliability growth or degradation trends during testing
   - May not be appropriate for all failure processes (e.g., wear-out failures)
   - Violations of exponential assumption can lead to incorrect boundary classifications

5. **Limited Diagnostic Capability**
   - Does not identify root causes of failures or failure mechanisms
   - Provides no insight into failure types, severity, or defect distribution
   - Cannot guide targeted improvement efforts or prioritize fixes
   - Treats all failures as equivalent (no weighting by severity)
   - No decomposition by subsystem, module, or failure mode

6. **Data Transformation Issues**
   - Original grouped interval data required transformation assumptions
   - Uniform distribution assumption may not reflect actual failure timing
   - Transformation introduces approximation errors and information loss
   - Alternative transformations could yield different results
   - Zero-failure intervals create special handling requirements

7. **Lack of Contextual Factors**
   - Does not account for varying test intensity or operational profiles
   - Cannot incorporate covariates (test effort, code changes, environmental factors)
   - Assumes homogeneous testing conditions throughout
   - May miss important relationships between failures and external factors

8. **Tool and Formula Variability**
   - Different RDC tools may implement different boundary formulas
   - Standard textbook formulas differ from proprietary tool implementations
   - Lack of standardization complicates reproducibility
   - Results may not be comparable across different analysis tools

### Synthesis

**Use RDC when**:

- Testing resources are limited and quick accept/reject decisions are needed
- The target MTTF or FIO is well-defined and stakeholder-approved
- The system exhibits approximately constant failure rate (exponential distribution)
- Stakeholders prioritize clear acceptance criteria over detailed modeling
- Real-time feedback during testing is valuable for adaptive planning
- Historical precedent exists for similar systems (standard MTTF benchmarks)

**Not to rely solely on RDC when**:

- Predicting future reliability or residual defect counts is critical
- Understanding failure mechanisms and root causes is required
- The system exhibits obvious reliability growth or degradation trends
- Failure rate varies significantly over time or testing phases
- Diagnostic information is needed to guide improvement efforts
- Requirements are poorly defined or subject to negotiation

**Use with caution when**:

- Data requires significant transformation or aggregation
- Testing conditions change substantially during the observation period
- Failure severity varies widely (some failures are critical, others minor)
- The system is software-intensive with frequent updates

# Discussion on Similarity and Differences of the Two Techniques

## Fundamental Approaches

### Reliability Growth Testing

RGT views reliability as a dynamic process evolving over time. The method models how reliability changes as defects are discovered and corrected, with the fundamental question: "How is the system's reliability changing, and where is it headed?"

Key characteristics:

- Process-oriented and predictive
- Uses parametric models (NHPP-based)
- Provides continuous metrics (failure intensity, MVF)
- Focuses on trends and future states

### Reliability Demonstration Chart

RDC views reliability assessment as a threshold-based decision problem. The method tests whether observed performance meets specified requirements, with the fundamental question: "Does the system meet the MTTF threshold?"

Key characteristics:

- Threshold-based and descriptive
- Uses decision boundaries from hypothesis testing
- Provides binary decisions (Accept/Reject/Continue)
- Focuses on current state against requirements

## Key Similarities

Both methods share several important characteristics:

1. **Time-based analysis**: Both require temporal ordering of failures and analyze cumulative behavior over the testing period.

2. **Statistical inference**: Both employ probability models and provide probabilistic rather than deterministic conclusions.

3. **Decision support**: Both inform release readiness decisions and help determine when testing is sufficient.

4. **Independence assumption**: Both assume failures are independent events and corrections do not introduce new defects.

## Key Differences

### Scope and Purpose

**RGT** is predictive, forecasting future failure behavior and supporting test planning. It extrapolates beyond observed data to estimate when reliability goals will be achieved.

**RDC** is descriptive, evaluating whether observed performance meets thresholds. It does not forecast future behavior or estimate remaining defects.

### Model Complexity

**RGT** requires selecting among competing parametric models (Goel-Okumoto, Generalized Model, etc.) and estimating multiple parameters. Model selection and goodness-of-fit assessment require statistical expertise.

**RDC** uses a standard threshold comparison method requiring only the MTTF value. No model selection or parameter estimation is needed, making it more accessible to non-specialists.

### Underlying Assumptions

**RGT** explicitly models time-varying failure intensity, assuming reliability growth during active debugging. It is designed for systems undergoing improvement.

**RDC** assumes constant failure rate (exponential distribution). It is designed for systems in stable operational states.

### Data Requirements

**RGT** typically requires 20-30 or more failures for stable parameter estimation and works directly with interval-based data.

**RDC** can make decisions with as few as 5-10 failures but requires time-between-failure data, which may necessitate transformation.

### Outputs

**RGT** produces continuous metrics including failure intensity functions, mean value functions, and growth rates. These outputs are rich in diagnostic information but require interpretation.

**RDC** produces discrete region classifications (Accept/Reject/Continue). These outputs provide clear decisions but limited diagnostic insight.

### Covariate Handling

**RGT** can incorporate covariates (execution effort, test coverage) to model how reliability depends on testing conditions. This enables scenario analysis and causal understanding.

**RDC** treats all failures uniformly without accounting for contextual factors. It assumes homogeneous testing conditions throughout.

## When to Use Each Method

### Use RGT when

- Active development with ongoing debugging is occurring
- Prediction of when reliability goals will be achieved is needed
- Understanding what drives reliability changes is important
- Planning resource allocation and test duration
- Comparing competing systems or design alternatives
- Estimating residual defect counts

### Use RDC when

- Acceptance testing with predetermined MTTF requirements
- Quick go/no-go decisions during testing
- Real-time monitoring with incremental updates
- Communication to non-technical stakeholders
- Systems in stable operational mode
- Limited failure data is available (early testing)

### Use Both when

- Comprehensive reliability assessment is required
- Cross-validation of findings is valuable
- Different stakeholder audiences need different perspectives
- Risk mitigation through complementary methods is desired

## Why Both Methods Were Used in This Assignment

The combined use of RGT and RDC provided several benefits:

**Cross-validation**: RDC independently confirmed RGT's identification of the reliability shock period, strengthening confidence in the finding.

**Comprehensive perspective**: RGT revealed why reliability behaved as it did (covariates E, F), while RDC revealed how close performance came to failure thresholds.

**Balanced assessment**: RGT's optimism about the mature testing phase was balanced by RDC's conservatism about overall margin, preventing both overconfidence and excessive pessimism.

**Stakeholder communication**: Technical teams benefit from RGT's detailed analysis, while management benefits from RDC's clear accept/reject status.

**Methodological robustness**: Different assumptions (growth vs. constant rate) and paradigms (modeling vs. hypothesis testing) were tested, with convergent findings having higher credibility.

## Complementary Strengths

Neither method is universally superior. Their value depends on the context and objectives:

- For active development phases, RGT provides richer insights into trends and improvement drivers.
- For acceptance testing, RDC provides clearer threshold-based decisions.
- For comprehensive assessment, using both methods (as in this assignment) provides a robust, multi-faceted understanding that neither alone could deliver.

The assignment demonstrates best practice by leveraging complementary techniques to build confidence in conclusions through multiple independent lines of evidence.

# Difficulties encountered, challenges overcome, and lessons learned

### 1. Running `C-SFRAT` Linux version on macOS 26

We faced the difficulty of running `C-SFRAT` v1.0 on our macOS 26 machine. With the help of Gemini, we were able to run the Linux executable of `C-SFRAT` v1.0 on macOS 26 using Docker and XQuartz. The steps are documented [here](/run-C-SFRAT-macos-26.md).

### 2. Handling grouped failure data for analysis

The provided dataset consisted of failures aggregated per interval, whereas several analysis techniques (such as Reliability Demonstration Chart) require time-between-failure data. To address this, we assumed a uniform distribution of failures within each interval to approximate time-based data. This introduced an approximation but allowed us to proceed with the analysis in a consistent manner.**Lesson Learned:** Data preprocessing plays a critical role in reliability analysis, and assumptions made during transformation must be carefully justified.

### 3. Identifying a valid data range for modelling

The initial dataset showed inconsistent behaviour, making it difficult to directly apply reliability models. Using the Laplace factor, we observed that the full dataset did not exhibit strong reliability growth, whereas the segmented range (intervals 19–31) showed statistically significant improvement. This led to selecting a stable subset of data for further analysis. **Lesson Learned:** Not all data is suitable for modelling. Statistical validation is necessary to ensure that the selected dataset reflects a consistent reliability trend.

### 4. Interpreting sudden spikes in failure behaviour

A noticeable increase in failures was observed during the testing period, which initially appeared as a degradation in system reliability. However, further analysis indicated that this spike was due to increased testing effort, uncovering previously hidden defects. **Lesson Learned:** Failure spikes should be interpreted in context. Increased failure detection can indicate improved testing effectiveness rather than system deterioration.

### 5. Model selection and interpretation

Multiple models were evaluated, and selecting the most appropriate ones required careful comparison using statistical metrics. Different models produced slightly different results, making it necessary to focus on consistency and overall fit.**Lesson Learned:** Reliability analysis is model-dependent, and conclusions should be supported by consistent behavior across well-performing models.

### 6. Defining a target failure rate

The assignment did not provide a predefined target failure rate, which made decision-making less straightforward. We addressed this by deriving a practical target (approximately 1.5–2 failures per interval) from the stabilized portion of the failure intensity plot.**Lesson Learned:** In the absence of predefined thresholds, it is important to derive data-driven criteria and justify them clearly.

### 7. Using RDC with transformed data

Applying the Reliability Demonstration Chart required converting the dataset into a compatible format and selecting appropriate MTTF thresholds. Small changes in these parameters significantly affected the acceptance decision. **Lesson Learned:** RDC is sensitive to assumptions and input parameters, and results should be interpreted with caution.

### 8. Integrating multiple reliability techniques

Combining insights from Reliability Growth Testing and RDC required aligning different perspectives on reliability, as one focuses on trends while the other focuses on threshold-based decisions. **Lesson Learned:** Different reliability methods provide complementary insights, and understanding how to combine them is essential for a comprehensive analysis.
